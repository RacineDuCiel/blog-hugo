Core Objective 4.1: Apply Appropriate Risk Strategies 1. Foundational Risk Terminology

  • Asset: Item of value (tangible or intangible) to an organization (e.g., data, hardware, software, reputation, property).
  • Risk: Probability/likelihood of a threat exploiting a vulnerability.
  • Vulnerability: Weakness in hardware, software, or components exploitable by a threat.
  • Threat: Agent, condition, or circumstance potentially causing harm, loss, or compromise to an asset.
  • Motivation: Driving force behind a threat actor’s activity (e.g., money, fame, challenge).
  • Risk Source: Origin of risk (Internal: employee error, hardware failure; External: natural disaster, protests).
  • Common Threats:
    • Natural Disasters (weather, fire, flood, pandemic)
    • Malicious Code (viruses, worms, Trojans)
    • Breach of Physical Security (insider/outsider intrusion, theft)
    • Hacker Attack (data theft, modification, damage)
    • Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) (overwhelming systems with traffic, often via botnets)
    • Cyberterrorism (attacking critical national infrastructure)

⠀2. The Risk Assessment Process

  • Purpose: Identify weaknesses, prioritize protection for critical areas, establish a security baseline, evaluate likelihood & impact, determine response, monitor progress.
  • Key Steps: 1 Asset Identification & Valuation: Identify all assets (tangible & intangible). Consider acquisition cost, liability, production cost impact, value to competitors, criticality. Difficult process, often requires expertise. 2 Information Classification: Categorize data based on CIA triad impact to determine handling requirements.
    • Government System (Confidentiality Focus):
    • Top Secret: Grave damage to national security.
    • Secret: Serious damage.
    • Confidential: Damage.
    • Unclassified: Not sensitive (may have FOUO designation).
    • Commercial System (Integrity & Confidentiality Focus):
    • Confidential: Most sensitive, competitive advantage.
    • Private: Personal nature (HR, medical).
    • Sensitive: Requires controls, damage if lost/altered.
    • Public: No damage if disclosed. 3 Risk Assessment Execution: Identify potential risks/threats, determine likelihood and impact. Usually performed by a cross-functional Risk Management Team (needs senior management support, funding, clear goals). Data sources: audits, business owners, HR, legal, IT/Sec admins, etc. 4 Risk Analysis Options (Quantitative vs. Qualitative):
    • Quantitative Risk Assessment:
    • Method: Assigns monetary values ($) to risk elements.
    • Goal: Calculate financial loss to justify controls.
    • Key Formulas:
    • SLE (Single Loss Expectancy) = AV (Asset Value) * EF (Exposure Factor)
    • EF: Percentage of asset value lost if threat occurs.
    • ALE (Annualized Loss Expectancy) = SLE * ARO (Annualized Rate of Occurrence)
    • ARO: Estimated frequency (times per year) a threat is expected to occur.
    • Strengths: Clear financial impact, easy for management to understand.
    • Weaknesses: Time-consuming, hard to assign $ value to all elements, relies on estimations/history (Stochastic events are hard to predict). Often uses automated tools & trend analysis.
    • Qualitative Risk Assessment:
    • Method: Ranks risks using non-monetary scales (e.g., Low, Medium, High) based on scenarios, intuition, experience.
    • Goal: Prioritize risks based on subjective severity/likelihood. Often uses CIA impact ratings (NIST SP 800-53, FIPS 199).
    • Examples: ISAM, Delphi Technique, FRAP, FMEA/FMECA.
    • Strengths: Faster, handles non-financial impacts.
    • Weaknesses: Subjective, lacks precise cost justification preferred by management. Often uses a Risk-Ranking Matrix (Severity vs. Frequency). 5 Risk Determination & Response (Handling): Decide action based on assessment results and Risk Appetite (organization’s overall willingness to accept risk) vs. Risk Tolerance (acceptable deviation for a specific risk). 6 Implementing Controls: Put measures in place based on the assessment report and decisions.

⠀3. Risk Handling Techniques (AATM)

  • Avoid: Eliminate the activity or process causing the risk. Stop doing it. (May have opportunity cost).
  • Accept: Understand the risk, evaluate it, and decide the benefits outweigh the costs. Requires informed decision by management.
  • Transfer: Shift the risk to a third party (e.g., Insurance, Outsourcing). Incurs ongoing cost.
  • Mitigate: Implement controls (physical, technical, operational) to reduce the likelihood or impact of the risk. Most common approach.
  • Other Concepts:
    • Residual Risk: Risk remaining after controls are implemented. (Cannot eliminate all risk).
    • Inherent Risk: Risk present before controls are applied; risk associated with performing an activity at all.
    • Risk Exception: Intentionally not mitigating a risk normally considered unacceptable, requires formal documentation, approval, and monitoring per policy.
    • Risk Deterrence: Discouraging exploitation through policies, laws, or consequences (e.g., legal penalties for hacking).

⠀4. Implementing Controls

  • Driven by the Risk Assessment Report.
  • Align controls with Information Classification and CIA requirements.
  • Consider Total Cost of Ownership (TCO): Purchase price + operational costs (maintenance, support, updates, etc.).
  • Aim for greatest risk reduction at lowest ROI/Cost-Benefit.
  • Translate technical risks into business terms for management.
  • Types of Controls:
    • Physical: Locks, fences, guards, CCTV, lighting.
    • Technical (Logical): Encryption, VPNs, Firewalls, IDS/IPS, Access Control Lists.
    • Operational (Administrative): Policies, procedures, training, hiring practices, BCP/DRP.
  • Requires budget, prioritization, planning, and ongoing security awareness training for users.

⠀5. Policies Used to Manage Employees

  • Goal: Protect the organization via structure and control throughout the employee lifecycle.
  • Pre-Employment:
    • Background Checks
    • Education Verification
  • Employment:
    • Initial Training
    • Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) - Signed
    • Nondisclosure Agreement (NDA) - Signed
    • Employee Handbook (Code of conduct, policies, security practices)
    • Key Employee Controls:
      • Separation of Duties: Split critical tasks among multiple people.
      • Job Rotation: Move employees between roles (detects fraud, cross-training).
      • Mandatory Vacations: Forces others to cover role, potentially uncovering irregularities (common in finance).
      • Least Privilege: Grant only necessary access/permissions required for the job.
      • Need to Know: Access granted only to information specifically required for duties.
      • Dual Control: Requires two individuals to perform a critical action together (related: M of N Control).
    • Ongoing Training & Awareness: Crucial for reinforcing policy, recognizing threats. Tailor to audience (technical vs. non-technical). Annual reinforcement needed.
  • End of Employment (Termination):
    • Defined procedures for voluntary/involuntary separation.
    • Timely communication between HR, Management, and IT Security.
    • Immediate termination of access (physical & logical).
    • Return of company assets (keys, laptops, phones, badges).
    • Exit Interview.
    • Reminder of NDAs/Non-competes.

⠀6. Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)

  • Purpose: Determine if a planned action/control is acceptable by comparing costs vs. benefits.
  • Key Metrics:
    • ROI (Return on Investment) = Net Profit / Total Assets (Requires good asset management).
    • TCO (Total Cost of Ownership): Full cost over lifecycle.
    • Payback Analysis: Time until benefits exceed costs.
  • Considers tangible and intangible benefits.

⠀7. Continuous Monitoring

  • Purpose: Evaluate control effectiveness on an ongoing (near real-time) basis. Detect failures quickly.
  • Driven by changes (config, environment, personnel).
  • NIST SP 800-37 Components: Config management, control processes, security impact analyses, assessments, status reporting, owner involvement.
  • Updates the Risk Register: Central log of identified risks, assessment, ownership, response, and status throughout the lifecycle.

⠀8. Enterprise Security Architecture (ESA) Frameworks & Governance

  • Purpose: Plan, allocate, control security resources; align IT/Security with business strategy. (People, Process, Technology).
  • Examples: EA (Federal), SABSA.
  • Goal: Measure performance, ensure ROI on security.
  • Common Risk Management Frameworks (RMFs): FISMA, COSO ERM, ISO 31000.
  • Balances technical/procedural solutions for long-term efficiency.

⠀9. Metrics and Analysis

  • Metric: A measured value (e.g., network utilization %, server uptime).
  • KPI (Key Performance Indicator): Metric tied to a goal (“How are we doing?”). Measures progress towards objectives (e.g., >99.9% uptime). Relates to Scalability, Availability, Reliability.
  • KRI (Key Risk Indicator): Metric indicating level of risk exposure (“How careful must we be?”). Monitors risk levels relative to tolerance.
  • Trend Data: Analyze historical data (internal or external like FBI IC3) to anticipate threats.

⠀10. Analyzing Security Solutions for Business Needs

  • Evaluate solutions based on:
    • Performance: Speed, accuracy, completeness (Uptime Agreements, TSF, Abandon Rate, FCR).
    • Latency: Delay tolerance (critical for VoIP, less for FTP).
    • Scalability: Ability to handle growth.
    • Capability: Ability to meet specific goals.
    • Usability vs. Security: Finding balance (Trade-off Analysis). Controls must be effective and usable.
    • Recoverability: Ability to restore to a specific point-in-time.
    • Maintainability: Ease of upkeep over expected lifespan.
    • Availability: Uptime, often measured by MTBF & MTTR.

⠀Core Objective 4.4: Importance of BCP and DR Concepts 1. Key Concepts & Planning

  • BCP (Business Continuity Planning): Strategy to maintain essential functions during any interruption (minor or major). Based on BIA.
  • BIA (Business Impact Analysis): Formal process to identify Mission Essential Functions and the critical systems supporting them. Determines impact of outages.
  • DRP (Disaster Recovery Plan): Specific plan activated by a disaster to recover IT systems and infrastructure. Subset of BCP. Focuses often on recovery after an event.
  • Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA): Assesses privacy risks associated with processing personal information for a new project or system.
  • Key Metrics:
    • RPO (Recovery Point Objective): Maximum tolerable data loss measured in time (e.g., backups every hour means RPO is <= 1 hour).
    • RTO (Recovery Time Objective): Maximum tolerable downtime for restoring a function/system after disruption.
    • MTTR (Mean Time To Recover/Repair): Average time taken to restore a failed system/component.
    • MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures): Average time a device operates before failing.
    • RSL (Recovery Service Level): Percentage of normal production capacity/performance needed during recovery (0-100%).

⠀2. Recovery Sites

  • Chosen based on RTO/RPO needs and budget.
  • Cold Site: Facility with space, power, HVAC; no hardware or data. Cheapest, longest RTO. Requires full setup.
  • Warm Site: Facility with hardware installed & configured; data needs restoration (backups). Moderate cost & RTO.
  • Hot Site: Fully operational duplicate environment with current/near-current data replication. Most expensive, shortest RTO. Minimal effort to switch over.
  • Mobile Site: Transportable facility (e.g., trailer) with necessary infrastructure.

⠀3. Incident Response Plan (IRP)

  • Procedures for handling security incidents/breaches.
  • Defines Roles/Responsibilities.
  • Includes reporting, containment, eradication, recovery steps.
  • After-Action Reports / Lessons Learned: Review post-incident to improve IRP, policies, training.

⠀4. Testing Plans

  • Validate effectiveness of BCP/DRP/IRP.
  • Checklist: Review plan documentation for completeness/accuracy.
  • Walk-Through: Team verbally steps through the plan.
  • Tabletop Exercise: Discussion-based simulation of a scenario; focuses on roles, responsibilities, decision-making.
  • Parallel Test / Simulation Test: Activate recovery systems/processes alongside production (no production impact). Tests capability.
  • Full Interruption Test: Shut down production systems and failover to recovery site. Highest assurance, highest risk.

⠀5. Reviewing Existing Security Control Effectiveness

  • Gap Analysis: Compares current state (“where we are”) to desired state (“where we want to be”). Identifies deficiencies.
  • Audits (Internal/External): Formal review of controls, policies, procedures against standards (e.g., COBIT, FISMA, GAAP, SAS) or internal benchmarks.
    • Internal: Performed by employees (or hired firm) for management assurance/improvement.
    • External: Performed by independent third party, often for compliance/statutory reasons. Frequency driven by policy/regulation.
  • Vulnerability Assessments: Use tools (Nessus, Nmap, OpenVAS) to scan for known weaknesses. Provides snapshot; can have false positives/negatives.
  • Ethical Hacking / Penetration Testing: Simulates attacker methods to test defenses and discover exploitable vulnerabilities.

⠀6. Lessons Learned / After-Action Reviews

  • Formal process after projects, incidents, or contract implementations (SLAs).
  • Purpose: Identify successes, failures, and areas for improvement in policies, procedures, technology, communication, training.
  • Informs updates to plans and processes.

⠀7. Problem Solving

  • Use judgment and experience for complex issues.
  • General Process: 1 Define the Problem (Symptoms) 2 Gather Facts (Did it work before? What changed? When? Who else?) 3 Brainstorm Possibilities 4 Implement & Test Solutions (One change at a time) 5 Evaluate & Document (Share knowledge)

⠀Exam Essentials Summary:

  • Know Qualitative (subjective, ranked) vs. Quantitative (monetary, calculated) risk assessment.
  • Understand how to determine risk (likelihood/impact) and decide how to handle it (Accept, Avoid, Mitigate, Transfer - AATM).
  • Realize controls cost money; justify them via risk assessment (cost vs. benefit/ALE).
  • BCP/DR are critical for survival; understand BIA, RPO, RTO, site types, and testing.
  • Risk assessment is ongoing; frameworks help structure the process.
  • Policies (HR, IT) and Training are crucial administrative controls.
  • Analyze metrics (KPIs, KRIs) and trends to manage risk and performance.
  • Continuously monitor and test controls (Audits, Vuln Scans, Pen Tests).
