Core Objective: Given a scenario, perform threat management activities. 1. Threat Management Concepts

  • Cyber Intelligence: Understanding threats, weaknesses, risks, and response readiness. Essential for proactive defense.
  • Threat Hunting: Proactive search for undiscovered threats/issues within the environment (e.g., insider threats, shadow IT, misconfigurations, outdated processes). Goes beyond signature-based tools. Requires perseverance, creativity, patience, vigilance.
  • Threat Emulation: Imitating threat actions/interactions with vulnerabilities to better understand and mitigate risks.

⠀2. Intelligence Types

  • Operational Intelligence: Focuses on maintaining business continuity. Identifies ongoing trends and systemic risks. Includes threat hunting and threat emulation to create usable defenses (e.g., signatures) before incidents occur.
  • Tactical Intelligence: Focuses on the “here and now.” Used for understanding and responding to specific, immediate discoveries (e.g., finding commodity malware during incident response).
    • Commodity Malware: Publicly available, non-customized malicious code used by many actors.
  • Strategic Intelligence: Looks further out. Asks “Who/what will threaten us next?” Considers future targeted attacks based on industry or organizational value (e.g., industrial espionage).

⠀3. Threat Actor Types & Properties

  • FBI Broad Categories: Organized Crime (most common), State-Sponsored, Cyberterrorist.
  • Common Actor Types:
    • Nation-State / APT / Cyberterrorists: Backed by countries. Goals: Intel gathering, data theft, accessing advanced tech. Often use Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs) to maintain long-term access after compromise. May target critical infrastructure (ICS/SCADA). Examples: GhostNet, Stuxnet, Shady RAT.
    • Organized Crime: Primarily motivated by money. Target banks, finance, companies, users. Goals: Steal PII, credit cards, identities. Often well-resourced (time, money, skills), persistent, and capable of creating zero-days. Examples: LoJax, Bad Rabbit, NotPetya, Colonial Pipeline, JBS Foods attacks.
    • Insider Threat (Disgruntled Employees): Highly dangerous due to existing trust, knowledge (policies, structure, processes), and access.
    • Competitors: Target intellectual property (IP).
    • Hacktivist: Hack for a political or social cause. Examples: Anonymous, LulzSec.
    • Script Kiddies: Limited skills, rely on downloaded automated tools, opportunistic targets.
  • Hacker Categories (Ethics):
    • Authorized (White Hat): Ethical hackers, perform authorized tests with permission.
    • Unauthorized (Black Hat): Criminal attackers (greed, revenge, challenge).
    • Semi-authorized (Gray Hat): Operate near legal boundaries, sometimes cross over.
  • Threat Actor Properties:
    • Resources: Key differentiators include Skill, Time, and Money. Nation-state & organized crime often have significant amounts.
    • Supply Chain Access: Attacking a target by compromising a trusted third-party supplier or service they rely on. Difficult to mitigate technically. Examples: Stuxnet, Target (2013), British Airways (2018), SolarWinds (2020), Microsoft Exchange (2021).
    • Capabilities/Sophistication: Ability to leverage vulnerabilities, create exploits (zero-days).

⠀4. Intelligence Collection Methods

  • Open-Source Intelligence (OSINT): Gathering information from publicly available sources using intelligence techniques.
    • Sources: Databases, publications, patents, social media, WHOIS, search engines, routing tables, DNS records, intelligence feeds (often proprietary but based on OSINT).
    • Social Media: Reveals personal details, affiliations, potential leverage points for social engineering.
    • Deep Web: Part of the internet not indexed by standard search engines. Information source (use skepticism). Different from Dark Web.
    • Dark Web: Not indexed, focus on anonymity, hosts illegal markets (e.g., for commodity malware).
    • WHOIS: Provides domain registration and technical contact information.
    • Routing Tables: Reveal network paths and known subnets (found on routers, L3 switches, hosts).
    • DNS Records: Provide info on domains, subdomains, IP blocks, services. Mitigation: Audit zones, patch servers, hide BIND version, restrict zone transfers, disable recursion, use isolated servers, DDoS mitigation, 2FA.
  • Human Intelligence (HUMINT): Gathering information through direct human interaction, building rapport, deception. Often part of Social Engineering (which also includes non-personal methods like phishing emails, calls, texts). Focuses on leveraging trust.

⠀5. Threat Analysis Frameworks

  • MITRE ATT&CK (Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge): A comprehensive, matrix-based framework categorizing attacker TTPs.
    • Enterprise Matrix Tactics: Reconnaissance -> Resource Development -> Initial Access -> Execution -> Persistence -> Privilege Escalation -> Defense Evasion -> Credential Access -> Discovery -> Lateral Movement -> Collection -> Command and Control -> Exfiltration -> Impact. (Know the general flow).
    • Exists for Enterprise (all OS), specific OS (Win, Lin, Mac), Mobile.
  • MITRE ATT&CK for ICS: Tailored for Industrial Control Systems/SCADA. Supplements, does not replace, the Enterprise matrix. Focuses on ICS-specific tactics/techniques.
    • ICS Tactics: Collection -> Command and Control -> Discovery -> Evasion -> Execution -> Impact -> Impair Process Control -> Inhibit Response Function -> Initial Access -> Lateral Movement -> Persistence. (Note differences/overlap with Enterprise).
  • Cyber Kill Chain (Lockheed Martin): A linear model describing stages of an attack. Shorter/more concise than ATT&CK.
    • Steps: Reconnaissance -> Weaponization -> Delivery -> Exploitation -> Installation -> Command & Control (C2) -> Actions on Objectives.
  • Diamond Model of Intrusion Analysis: A non-linear model for organizing threat intel data around an event. Helps analyze relationships.
    • Four Corners: Adversary (Who), Capability (How), Infrastructure (Where), Victim (Target). (Know these four points).

⠀Core Objective: Given a scenario, analyze indicators of compromise and formulate an appropriate response. 6. Indicators of Compromise (IoCs)

  • Definition: Evidence suggesting a security incident has occurred, requiring investigation.
  • Sources:
    • Logs:
      • Network Logs: From routers/switches. Show abnormal traffic patterns, sources/destinations, rule triggers. Monitor perimeter & internal nets.
      • Vulnerability Logs: Scan results. Provide baseline for comparison.
      • Operating System (OS) Logs: Host events, errors, system info (Windows Event Viewer: App/Sec/System logs; Linux: /var/log via syslogd/rsyslogd).
      • Access Logs: Track user logins (who, what, when, where).
      • NetFlow Logs: Cisco protocol. Collects IP traffic statistics via router interfaces for analysis (collector/analyzer tools). Useful for intrusion analysis. (sFlow is an alternative - sampled flow).
    • Alerts & Notifications:
      • FIM (File Integrity Monitoring) Alerts: Signal unauthorized changes to monitored files.
      • Antivirus Alerts: Endpoint detection of known malware signatures.
      • IDS/IPS Alerts: Triggered by matching suspicious network/host activity against rules. Often feed into SIEM.
      • SIEM Alerts: Centralized alerts correlated from multiple sources.
      • DLP (Data Loss Prevention) Alerts: Indicate potential data exfiltration based on rules monitoring outbound traffic (often uses deep packet inspection at network boundary).
    • Packet Capture (PCAP): Raw network traffic data (e.g., using WinDump/tcpdump) for detailed analysis.
    • Unusual Process Activity: Often detected by HIDS/anomaly detection.
    • Threat Intelligence Feeds / File Repositories: External sources providing known bad file signatures, log patterns, etc.

⠀7. Response Mechanisms & Rules

  • General Approach: Avoid panic, gather more information, make informed response.
  • Firewalls: Hardware/software devices controlling traffic between network segments (trusted/untrusted).
    • Placement: Typically at network boundaries (perimeter).
    • Host-Based Firewalls: Run on individual endpoints, protect that specific host (defense-in-depth).
  • Firewall Rules / Access Control Lists (ACLs):
    • Function: Define what traffic is allowed, blocked, logged, or analyzed based on criteria. Basic data flow enforcement.
    • Processing: Top-down order; first match wins. Implicit deny all at the end.
    • Standard ACLs (Cisco): Filter based on source IP address only. access-list <#> {permit|deny} {host |source |any}
    • Extended ACLs (Cisco): Filter based on source/destination IP, protocol, source/destination port, flags (e.g., TCP established), DSCP, ToS, etc. More granular.
    • Importance: Crucial for network segmentation and access control. Need secure configuration/baselining first. Understand how rule order and specificity impact traffic flow.
  • IPS/IDS Rules:
    • Signature Rules: Match traffic/activity against known malicious patterns.
    • Behavior Rules (Anomaly): Compare activity against a baseline of normal behavior; alert on deviations.
  • DLP Rules: Define sensitive data patterns/types and policies for blocking/alerting on outbound transmission.
  • Scripts/Regular Expressions (Regex): Used for pattern matching (historically in IDS/AV, still used in custom scripts/analysis). Regex matching can be resource-intensive.

⠀Exam Essentials Summary:

  • Intel Gathering: Know OSINT sources (web, social media, WHOIS, DNS, etc.) vs. HUMINT (direct interaction, social engineering).
  • IoCs: Recognize what constitutes an IoC and where to find them (Logs: Network, OS, Access, Vuln, NetFlow; Alerts: FIM, AV, IDS/IPS, SIEM, DLP; PCAP; Unusual Activity).
  • Threat Actors: Understand motivations, resources, and common TTPs of different types (Nation-State/APT, Organized Crime, Insider, Hacktivist, Script Kiddie, Competitor). Be aware of supply chain risks.
  • Frameworks: Know the purpose and basic structure/steps of ATT&CK (Enterprise & ICS), Cyber Kill Chain, and Diamond Model.
  • Response (ACLs): Understand the purpose, syntax (basic vs. extended), processing order (top-down, implicit deny), and impact of ACLs on network traffic flow. Be able to analyze simple ACL examples.
